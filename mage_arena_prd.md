# Mage Arena — PRD (Hackathon Demo)

**Project:** Tongue-Twister Mage Arena
**Format:** Hot-seat two-player duel on a single laptop
**Build window:** Hackathon (target: working demo by submission)
**Author:** Athul
**Status:** Draft v1

---

## 1. One-line pitch

A turn-based magic duel where the spells are AI-generated tongue twisters, and how cleanly you pronounce them out loud determines whether you blast your opponent or blast yourself.

## 2. Why this concept works for a hackathon

Three reasons it demos well in front of judges:

The mechanic is legible in five seconds. Two people sit at a laptop, see three spell cards, hear them read aloud, take turns saying them. No tutorial needed.

The AI angle is genuine, not bolted on. An LLM generates fresh spells every round (so no two demos are identical), produces the phonetic guide, and provides flavor text. Whisper handles speech-to-text. Both are doing real work on the critical path.

It produces demo-friendly moments. Someone fumbles "Sixth sheep's sheath shriek" on the hard spell, the spell backfires, the room laughs. That's the kind of moment that wins hackathons.

## 3. Goals and non-goals

### Goals

- Two humans can play a complete match end-to-end on one laptop, taking turns at the mic.
- AI generates three difficulty-tiered tongue twisters per round, with phonetic guides and brief flavor descriptions.
- The system reads spell names aloud at round start so both players know the target pronunciation.
- Speech is captured from the mic, transcribed, and scored against the target. The score determines whether the spell hits, partially hits, fizzles, or backfires.
- A single match runs in under 5 minutes — short enough to demo live multiple times.

### Non-goals (explicit cut list)

- No online multiplayer, no matchmaking, no accounts, no persistence across sessions.
- No mobile build. Laptop only — Chrome on macOS or Linux is the target.
- No fancy character art, no animated mage sprites, no spell VFX beyond simple HP-bar damage flashes. Visual fidelity is "clean and clear," not "rendered."
- No spell library, deckbuilding, or progression. Spells are generated fresh each round and forgotten.
- No anti-cheat, no rate limiting, no auth. Trust the players.
- No accessibility audit beyond keyboard/mouse working — voice-input by definition excludes some users, and that's accepted for the demo.

## 4. Target user and context

The user is a hackathon judge or fellow participant who walks up to the laptop with one other person. They have ~3 minutes of attention and zero patience for setup. They want to immediately understand what's happening and start playing.

The implication: no login screen, no name entry, no settings menu before the first match. Land on a "Tap to start" screen, hit it, go.

## 5. Core gameplay loop

Each match runs a fixed sequence of turns until one player's HP hits zero.

A turn looks like this. The active player sees three spell cards on screen — Easy, Medium, Hard — each with a name, a phonetic guide, damage value, and backfire risk. At the start of the round, the AI's voice reads each spell name aloud once so both players hear the canonical pronunciation. The active player picks a spell by clicking, then presses a "Cast" button to start mic recording. They speak the spell out loud, release the button (or wait for auto-stop), and the system transcribes their attempt, compares it to the target, and resolves the outcome. HP updates, then the laptop is passed to the other player for their turn.

Scoring tiers determine outcomes:

A score at or above the spell's threshold (75-85% depending on difficulty) is a perfect cast — full damage to the opponent. A score between 40% and the threshold is a partial cast — damage scales proportionally to accuracy. A score below 40% triggers a backfire roll based on the spell's risk percentage; a failed roll deals the spell's damage to the caster instead. If the backfire roll passes, the spell simply fizzles with no damage either way.

Both players start at 100 HP. Match ends when someone hits zero.

## 6. Spell design

Spells are English tongue twisters, generated by Claude or GPT at the start of each round. Three tiers per round, scaling difficulty by phonetic challenge rather than vocabulary obscurity.

### Easy tier

Short two-to-three word phrases with mild alliteration. Manageable with a single attempt. Example: "Slippery Shelf Shift" — `SLIP-er-ee SHELF SHIFT`. Damage 20, backfire risk 0%, perfect threshold 85%.

### Medium tier

Longer phrases with consonant cluster repetition. Demands deliberate articulation. Example: "Crisp Crisscross Crackle" — `KRISP KRIS-kraws KRAK-ul`. Damage 45, backfire risk 20%, perfect threshold 80%.

### Hard tier

Classic compound tongue twisters with sibilant or plosive overload. The kind that makes adults stumble. Example: "Sixth Sheep's Sheath Shriek" — `SIKSTH SHEEPS SHEETH SHREEK`. Damage 80, backfire risk 50%, perfect threshold 75%.

### Generation prompt structure

A single LLM call per round returns a JSON object with three spell entries. Each entry has: `name` (the spell), `phonetic` (a syllable-broken pronunciation guide for the user and the matcher), `damage`, `backfire_chance`, `threshold`, and `flavor` (a one-line evocative description). The system prompt instructs the model to favor plosives and sibilants, avoid proper nouns or rare vocabulary, and keep all spells under five words.

## 7. Pronunciation scoring

The scoring layer is the trickiest part to get feeling fair. The approach has three steps.

First, the recorded audio is sent to OpenAI's Whisper API for transcription. Whisper returns a plain text string of what the user said.

Second, both the transcript and the target spell name are normalized — lowercased, punctuation stripped, contractions expanded ("sheep's" → "sheeps"). This avoids penalizing a player for an apostrophe.

Third, similarity is computed using a weighted combination: 60% Levenshtein-based string similarity on the normalized transcript-vs-target, and 40% phoneme-level similarity using a phonetic algorithm (Metaphone or Double Metaphone via a JS library). The phoneme component is what distinguishes "sixth" from "sicks" — they're textually different but phonetically close, and a player who said "sicks sheep" should score higher than one who said "sixth potato."

The combined score is a 0-100 percentage. This is what's compared to the spell's threshold.

### Calibration concern

The threshold values (75-85%) are starting estimates. During the hackathon build, plan to spend at least one hour calibrating: have multiple people speak each spell tier, log the scores, and adjust thresholds so that "good faith attempt" reliably scores in the perfect band and "obvious flub" reliably falls below 40%. Without this pass, the game will feel either too easy (everyone perfects everything) or too punishing (everyone fizzles).

## 8. Spell narration (text-to-speech)

At the start of each round, the system reads each spell name aloud once so both players know the target pronunciation. This is non-negotiable — without it, players are guessing at how the AI expects them to say "Crisscross."

Implementation: OpenAI TTS or browser-native `SpeechSynthesis` API. Browser-native is free and good enough for a hackathon; the slight robotic quality actually fits the "AI mage announcer" framing. OpenAI TTS sounds better but adds latency and cost. Default to browser TTS; have OpenAI TTS as a fallback if browser voices sound bad on the demo machine.

## 9. UI screens

Five screens total. Anything more is over-scoped for a hackathon.

### Screen 1 — Title

A single "Tap to start" button. No name entry, no options.

### Screen 2 — Round intro

Briefly shown between rounds. Says "Round N — Player 1's turn" and plays the three spell name pronunciations in sequence. Auto-advances after audio finishes.

### Screen 3 — Spell selection

The screen the active player sees on their turn. Top: HP bars for both players. Middle: three spell cards, click-to-select. Bottom: a "Cast" button that activates after a spell is picked. The selected spell highlights with a colored border matching its tier (teal/amber/coral).

### Screen 4 — Casting

Replaces the spell grid the moment Cast is pressed. Shows a large mic icon, a recording indicator, and the selected spell's phonetic guide as a reading aid. Auto-stops recording after 4 seconds of silence or 8 seconds total, whichever comes first.

### Screen 5 — Resolution

After scoring completes, shows the outcome: pronunciation percentage, what Whisper heard ("You said: ..."), and the result tag (Perfect / Partial / Fizzled / Backfired). HP bars animate the damage. A "Pass to opponent" button advances to the next turn. Match-end shows a winner banner and a "Rematch" button.

## 10. Tech stack

The stack is chosen to minimize integration time. Everything runs locally with two API calls per round.

### Frontend

A single-page React app (Vite for fast dev). Tailwind for styling. Web Audio API + `MediaRecorder` for mic capture. Browser `SpeechSynthesis` for TTS. No state management library — `useState` is sufficient. Total frontend complexity: roughly one component per screen, a shared game state object, and a scoring utility module.

### Backend

A minimal FastAPI server (or none, if you prefer pure frontend). Two endpoints:

`POST /generate-spells` — calls Claude or GPT-4o, returns JSON with three spells.

`POST /transcribe` — accepts an audio blob, calls Whisper, returns the transcript string. Scoring happens client-side in JS so the result feels instant.

If skipping the backend, both calls go directly from the browser to the Anthropic and OpenAI APIs using user-supplied keys stored in `localStorage`. For a hackathon demo on your own laptop, this is fine. For showing it to others, the FastAPI proxy is safer.

### Libraries

`openai` (or direct fetch) for Whisper, `anthropic` (or direct fetch) for Claude, `js-levenshtein` for string similarity, `metaphone` or `double-metaphone` for phonetic similarity, `framer-motion` for the few small animations that make HP changes feel impactful.

## 11. Build plan and milestones

A four-milestone plan, each ending in something demonstrable.

### Milestone 1 — Static gameplay (target: half a day)

Hardcoded spells, no AI calls, no speech input. Two players click spell cards and "cast" them. Outcome is randomized within the spell's distribution. HP bars work, turn passing works, win condition works. This proves the gameplay loop end-to-end before any AI integration.

### Milestone 2 — Voice in (target: half a day)

Wire mic capture, send to Whisper, log the transcript to console. Implement scoring (Levenshtein + phonetic) against hardcoded targets. Replace the random outcome with the scored one. By end of this milestone, the game is fully playable with hardcoded spells but real voice input.

### Milestone 3 — AI spell generation (target: a few hours)

Replace hardcoded spells with the LLM call. Add round-start TTS narration. Now every round is fresh.

### Milestone 4 — Polish (target: remaining time)

Calibrate thresholds with real testers. Add HP-bar damage animations. Tighten visual design. Record a 30-second backup demo video in case the mic flakes during the live judge session.

If time runs out, drop Milestone 4 polish first, then Milestone 3 AI generation (revert to a hardcoded spell pool of ~30 tongue twisters). Voice input and the gameplay loop are the demo — those are the floor.

## 12. Risks and mitigations

The biggest risk is mic reliability during the live demo. Browser mic permissions, ambient noise, and Whisper latency can all sink the moment. Mitigations: test on the actual demo laptop ahead of time, have a wired headset mic on hand, and pre-record a backup demo video.

The second risk is scoring feeling unfair — the calibration problem mentioned earlier. Mitigation: budget explicit time for it, and add a hidden debug toggle to override scores during testing.

The third risk is LLM latency at round start. Generating spells could take 2-4 seconds, which feels long. Mitigation: pre-generate the next round's spells in the background while the current round is being played. By the time the round ends, the next set is ready.

A fourth, smaller risk: profanity or inappropriate output from the LLM. The system prompt should explicitly forbid this, and a simple word-filter pass on generated spells before display catches anything that slips through.

## 13. Success criteria for the demo

The demo succeeds if a judge can sit down with one other person, play a complete match in under five minutes without any explanation from the team, and have at least one moment where they laugh or react audibly to a botched pronunciation. That reaction is the product. Everything else exists to produce it.
